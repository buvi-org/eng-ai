Day 1 | 30 students | 10 hours

Wake up to the new reality

Day 1 should not feel like a lecture or a motivational seminar. It should make students experience the gap between college-style answers and real-world problem solving, then produce their first evidence of thinking, communication, and AI verification.

30students
5 groups6 students each
7 outputsby end of day

Day 1 intent

Reset expectations and capture baseline behavior.

The first day should tell students, through experience, that the course rewards observation, asking better questions, verifying AI, explaining clearly, finishing work, and improving after feedback.

Primary objectives

  • Make the AI wave feel real without creating fear or hype.
  • Show why software-only thinking is too narrow.
  • Introduce the course doctrine: problem first, reality first, AI as accelerator.
  • Measure baseline thinking, communication, AI usage, and coachability.
  • Start the daily evidence habit from the first day.
  • Begin identifying future roles: observer, builder, communicator, operator, analyst, leader.

Room design

With 30 students, structure matters.

Use five groups of six students. Each group needs a table, visible board or sheet, one nominated scribe, and one nominated presenter. Roles should rotate during the day.

5 groups × 6 students
1 lead facilitator
2-3 mentors or teaching assistants
Shared screen and timer
Printed worksheets or digital forms
Submission folder or form
AI access plan
Whiteboard or chart paper per group
Mentor scoring sheet

10-hour schedule

Day 1 is a sequence of short pressure tests.

Every block should produce evidence. Avoid long speeches. If students are passive, the day has failed.

09:00-09:45

Opening contract: what this course is and is not

Set the tone. This is not tuition, not a certificate factory, not a prompt class, and not a software-only startup camp. Students are here to become useful problem solvers.

  • 30-student activity: individual commitment card, then table-level discussion.
  • Output: signed learning contract plus one sentence: "I am here to become useful by..."
  • Mentor watches: who is attentive, cynical, confused, excited, passive.
09:45-10:45

AI wave reality map

Show practical examples across coding, CAD, customer support, manufacturing planning, data analysis, documentation, and business operations. The goal is sober awareness, not hype.

  • Group activity: each group lists 10 tasks AI will make cheaper and 10 human skills that become more valuable.
  • Output: AI impact map per group.
  • Mentor watches: whether students understand judgment, context, and verification.
10:45-11:00

Break

Mentors quickly mark early behavior signals while students pause.

11:00-12:15

Messy problem baseline challenge

Give all groups the same real-world case: a small fabrication shop is losing money because jobs are delayed, material is wasted, and customers keep calling for updates.

  • Group activity: identify symptoms, possible causes, actors, constraints, missing data, and first questions.
  • Rule: no solution proposals for the first 30 minutes.
  • Output: problem breakdown sheet.
  • Mentor watches: who jumps to apps, who asks questions, who sees operations, who sees money.
12:15-13:00

Group presentations: what is actually the problem?

Each group gets 4 minutes to present and 3 minutes of critique. The facilitator should challenge vague words like "efficiency", "management", and "AI solution".

  • Output: revised problem statement after critique.
  • Mentor watches: clarity, confidence, listening, correction after feedback.
13:00-14:00

Lunch plus informal observation

Mentors should sit separately and compare notes. Students should be told to notice one real operational issue during lunch.

  • Output: one lunch-time observation from each student.
  • Mentor watches: whether students continue observing outside formal blocks.
14:00-15:15

AI misuse test

Students ask AI to solve the fabrication shop case. Then they must identify what is generic, unverified, impractical, missing, or dangerous.

  • Individual activity: each student runs an AI query and marks weak points.
  • Group activity: combine findings into an AI error list.
  • Output: AI verification worksheet.
  • Mentor watches: blind trust vs healthy skepticism.
15:15-16:00

AI as coworker demonstration

Show how to ask AI for assumptions, missing data, interview questions, alternative causes, cost factors, and failure modes instead of asking for final answers.

  • Output: improved prompt and better question list.
  • Mentor watches: who learns to ask for thinking support instead of answer generation.
16:00-16:15

Break

Mentors flag students who need help with language, confidence, attention, or tool use.

16:15-17:15

Communication baseline

Each student explains the fabrication shop problem in simple language as if speaking to the shop owner. Keep it short and practical.

  • 30-student method: students pair up, record 90-second explanations, then each group selects one example to play.
  • Output: 90-second explanation video or written version.
  • Mentor watches: clarity, empathy, jargon control, confidence.
17:15-18:15

Daily build log setup and first evidence submission

Students create their daily log format. The log should capture what they did, what they learned, what they verified, what confused them, and what they will improve tomorrow.

  • Output: Day 1 build log submitted before leaving.
  • Mentor watches: completeness, honesty, ability to reflect.
18:15-19:00

Closing circle and Day 1 baseline scoring

Each group shares one changed belief. Facilitator previews Day 2: observation before solving. Mentors finish baseline notes.

  • Output: exit ticket: "One assumption I had that changed today."
  • Mentor watches: coachability, seriousness, emotional response to critique.

End-of-day evidence

By 19:00, each student should have submitted seven things.

Learning contract
Personal AI-wave reflection
Problem breakdown sheet
Revised problem statement
AI verification worksheet
90-second explanation
Day 1 build log
Lunch-time observation
Exit ticket

Mentor scoring

Day 1 is a baseline, not an exam.

The goal is to understand students. Do not shame weak students. Identify who needs language support, confidence support, tool support, or stronger challenge.

Score 1-5 on:

  • Curiosity
  • Problem framing
  • Assumption control
  • AI verification
  • Communication clarity
  • Participation and initiative
  • Coachability after critique
  • Evidence completion

Watch-outs

Common Day 1 failure modes.

Too much lecture and not enough student work.
Students think the course is only about AI tools.
Students jump directly to apps, dashboards, or chatbots.
Mentors do not record baseline observations.
Outputs are discussed but not submitted.
Weak students hide inside group work.
AI answers are accepted without verification.
The day feels motivational instead of operational.
