# Phase 1 Detailed Draft: Days 1-6

## Purpose

Days 1-6 should not try to teach everything. The first six days are for resetting the students' view of engineering, AI, problem solving, teamwork, and proof of work.

Many students will arrive with habits trained by college:

- Wait for instructions
- Search for the right answer
- Copy examples
- Avoid uncertainty
- Focus on marks instead of usefulness
- Use AI as an answer machine
- Underestimate real-world complexity

Phase 1 should break those habits gently but firmly. By Day 6, students should understand that the course rewards observation, thinking, building, evidence, communication, and improvement.

## Phase 1 Outcomes

By the end of Day 6, every student should have:

- A personal baseline profile
- A working AI learning and execution setup
- A daily build log habit
- A first visible evidence artifact on Day 1
- A first tiny useful real-world build by Day 4
- A problem observation notebook with at least 15 real-world observations
- Experience working in rotating mini-teams
- A basic understanding of AI as coworker, not answer machine
- A first mentor review
- A readiness score for Phase 2

The cohort team should have:

- Initial student segmentation
- Early risk list
- Communication ability map
- Thinking ability map
- Tool readiness map
- Candidate role hints for future teams
- Evidence that each student can complete daily work

## Before Day 1: Required Setup

Do not spend the first training day fighting logins and laptop problems. Before Day 1, the academy team should prepare:

- Student application data and contact list
- Attendance and daily log system
- Shared drive or LMS folder
- AI tool access plan
- WhatsApp/communication group
- Baseline challenge sheets
- Mentor scoring sheet
- Artifact submission method
- Photo/video permission policy
- Laptop and internet readiness checklist

Software accounts such as GitHub can be introduced when useful, but Phase 1 should not depend on every student becoming software-ready immediately.

## Day 1: Wake Up To The New Reality

**Day type:** Foundation day plus baseline day

**Core question:** Why is ordinary engineering education not enough anymore?

### Objectives

- Establish the seriousness and opportunity of the AI wave
- Explain that the course is problem-first, not software-first
- Make students understand the difference between knowledge, skill, judgment, and proof
- Capture baseline thinking, communication, and AI usage ability
- Start the daily evidence habit

### 10-Hour Shape

| Block | Activity | Output |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Opening orientation: what this course is and is not | Signed learning contract |
| 2 | AI wave discussion with examples from software, manufacturing, design, and business | Personal reflection note |
| 3 | Baseline challenge 1: break down a messy real-world problem | Problem breakdown sheet |
| 4 | Baseline challenge 2: explain a technical idea in simple language | 2-minute explanation video or live note |
| 5 | AI usage test: ask AI to solve something, then find what is wrong or incomplete | AI verification worksheet |
| 6 | Build log setup | Day 1 entry |
| 7 | Closing circle: what surprised you today? | Mentor observation notes |

### Suggested Case

"A small fabrication shop is losing money because jobs are delayed, material is wasted, and customers keep calling for status updates. What is actually the problem?"

Students must avoid jumping to "build software." They should identify possible causes:

- Bad estimation
- No job tracking
- No material planning
- Poor communication
- Machine downtime
- Rework
- Supplier delay
- No owner for each job

### Student Deliverables

- Personal reflection: "What skill do I think will matter after AI?"
- Baseline problem breakdown
- AI verification worksheet
- Day 1 build log

### Mentor Checks

- Does the student ask clarifying questions?
- Do they jump to solutions too quickly?
- Can they explain simply?
- Do they trust AI blindly?
- Are they active or passive?

### AI Monitoring Input

- Reflection note
- Problem breakdown
- AI transcript summary
- Mentor score: curiosity, clarity, verification, initiative

## Day 2: Learn To Observe Before Solving

**Day type:** Foundation day plus field-preparation day

**Core question:** Can you see problems clearly before trying to solve them?

### Objectives

- Teach observation as an engineering skill
- Introduce the difference between symptom, cause, constraint, and opportunity
- Train students to notice workflows, delays, waste, confusion, and repeated manual effort
- Begin local problem discovery without making business ideas yet

### 10-Hour Shape

| Block | Activity | Output |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Observation lecture: how to see work, not just objects | Observation checklist |
| 2 | Problem anatomy: symptom, root cause, actor, cost, frequency, workaround | Problem anatomy worksheet |
| 3 | Campus/local walk-through or simulated observation lab | 10 raw observations |
| 4 | Group sorting: which observations are real problems? | Ranked observation list |
| 5 | AI-assisted questioning: generate better questions, then filter them | Question bank |
| 6 | Mini-presentation: one observed problem in 90 seconds | Peer feedback |
| 7 | Build log and mentor notes | Day 2 entry |

### Observation Targets

Use what is accessible:

- Classroom attendance
- Lab equipment usage
- Canteen queue
- Workshop tool storage
- College transport
- Student project procurement
- Small shop inventory
- Fabrication job tracking
- Repair/maintenance work
- Parent/student admission processes

### Student Deliverables

- 10 raw observations
- 3 structured problem anatomy sheets
- 1 short presentation
- Day 2 build log

### Mentor Checks

- Can the student separate facts from assumptions?
- Can they describe who suffers and how often?
- Do they notice physical and operational constraints?
- Do they overuse AI language without evidence?

### AI Monitoring Input

- Observation list
- Structured problem sheets
- Presentation feedback
- Mentor score: observation, specificity, assumption control

## Day 3: AI As Coworker, Not Answer Machine

**Day type:** Skill sprint day

**Core question:** Can you use AI to think better without becoming dependent on it?

### Objectives

- Teach AI delegation, verification, and iteration
- Show why AI answers are often incomplete for real-world problems
- Train students to use AI for questions, checklists, alternatives, simulations, and documentation
- Establish AI usage rules for the course

### 10-Hour Shape

| Block | Activity | Output |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | AI coworker model: delegate, inspect, verify, improve | AI usage rules |
| 2 | Hallucination lab: students test AI on practical claims | Error log |
| 3 | Prompting for thinking: ask AI for assumptions, risks, alternatives | Improved problem framing |
| 4 | Verification sprint: check AI output using sources, calculations, or real observations | Verification evidence |
| 5 | Create personal AI workflow template | Personal AI workflow |
| 6 | Apply workflow to one Day 2 problem | Revised problem note |
| 7 | Build log and peer review | Day 3 entry |

### AI Usage Rules

- AI output is a draft, not truth
- Every important answer needs verification
- Ask AI for questions before asking for solutions
- Ask AI for assumptions and failure modes
- Use AI to compare options, not to avoid thinking
- Save useful AI conversations as evidence

### Student Deliverables

- AI error log
- Personal AI workflow template
- Revised problem note using AI responsibly
- Day 3 build log

### Mentor Checks

- Does the student verify AI claims?
- Can they improve a weak AI answer?
- Do they ask for alternatives?
- Can they explain why an AI answer is incomplete?

### AI Monitoring Input

- AI transcripts
- Verification evidence
- Before/after problem note
- Mentor score: AI delegation, verification, independence

## Day 4: First Tiny Real-World Build

**Day type:** Skill sprint day plus build day

**Core question:** Can you create a useful artifact in one day?

### Objectives

- Give students an early shipping win
- Show that a "build" does not always mean software
- Make students create something useful from an observed problem
- Teach minimum useful artifact thinking

### 10-Hour Shape

| Block | Activity | Output |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Minimum useful artifact examples | Artifact options list |
| 2 | Select one small problem from Day 2 | One-day build brief |
| 3 | Build sprint 1 | Draft artifact |
| 4 | Mentor review: is this useful or just decorative? | Revision notes |
| 5 | Build sprint 2 | Improved artifact |
| 6 | 2-minute demo | Demo feedback |
| 7 | Build log and evidence upload | Day 4 entry |

### Acceptable Tiny Builds

- Checklist for a repeated workshop process
- Job tracking board template
- Cost estimation spreadsheet
- Maintenance log format
- Simple inspection form
- WhatsApp message template for customer updates
- Small dashboard using sample data
- Manual workflow redesign
- Labeling system for tools/materials
- AI-generated but human-verified SOP
- Simple sensor/data capture concept sketch

### Not Acceptable

- Generic chatbot
- Generic todo app
- Landing page without real problem evidence
- AI-generated report with no verification
- Pretty slides with no operational usefulness

### Student Deliverables

- One-day build brief
- Artifact
- Demo
- Day 4 build log

### Mentor Checks

- Is the artifact connected to a real observation?
- Could a real person use it?
- Is the artifact too complicated?
- Did the student test it even lightly?

### AI Monitoring Input

- Build brief
- Artifact link or photo
- Demo feedback
- Mentor score: usefulness, completion, clarity

## Day 5: Teamwork, Roles, And Communication Under Pressure

**Day type:** Build day plus review day

**Core question:** Can students work with others without hiding, dominating, or waiting?

### Objectives

- Test students in rotating mini-teams
- Teach team roles before permanent team formation
- Practice communication, ownership, and conflict handling
- Build a slightly better artifact using multiple strengths

### 10-Hour Shape

| Block | Activity | Output |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Team behavior briefing: roles, ownership, decision logs | Team working agreement |
| 2 | Form rotating mini-teams of 3 | Team roster |
| 3 | Choose one problem from team observations | Team problem brief |
| 4 | Build/rework sprint | Team artifact v1 |
| 5 | Role switch: each student must contribute outside comfort zone | Contribution log |
| 6 | Peer review and critique | Peer feedback |
| 7 | Team demo and reflection | Day 5 entry |

### Rotating Roles

- Problem owner
- Reality checker
- AI operator
- Builder/documenter
- Presenter
- Cost/time estimator

For teams of 3, combine roles but ensure every student owns a visible piece.

### Student Deliverables

- Team problem brief
- Team artifact
- Individual contribution log
- Peer feedback
- Day 5 build log

### Mentor Checks

- Who takes initiative?
- Who listens well?
- Who hides?
- Who dominates?
- Who can translate messy discussion into action?
- Who communicates clearly?

### AI Monitoring Input

- Team artifact
- Individual contribution logs
- Peer feedback
- Mentor score: teamwork, ownership, communication, reliability

## Day 6: Baseline Review And Phase 2 Readiness Gate

**Day type:** Review day plus gate day

**Core question:** Is each student ready to enter deeper problem-solving work?

### Objectives

- Review all Phase 1 evidence
- Give each student a personal baseline profile
- Identify students needing extra support
- Capture early role strengths
- Set expectations for Phase 2
- Make the cohort feel the seriousness of weekly evidence

### 10-Hour Shape

| Block | Activity | Output |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Portfolio assembly: collect Day 1-5 evidence | Phase 1 evidence folder |
| 2 | Individual reflection: what changed in my thinking? | Reflection note |
| 3 | 3-minute individual review with mentor panel | Baseline score |
| 4 | Mini-demo fair: students show best artifact | Demo score |
| 5 | AI-generated progress summary review | Student profile draft |
| 6 | Phase 2 briefing: thinking like a builder | Phase 2 readiness plan |
| 7 | Catch-up clinic for weak submissions | Completion checklist |

### Baseline Scorecard

Score each student from 1 to 5 on:

- Curiosity
- Observation quality
- Problem framing
- AI usage maturity
- Verification habit
- Communication
- Build completion
- Team behavior
- Reliability
- Coachability

### Gate Decision

Students should be placed into one of three groups:

- **Ready:** can proceed normally into Phase 2
- **Needs support:** proceeds but gets extra clinic work
- **At risk:** requires parent/student conversation or probation plan

This is not to shame students. It is to prevent quiet failure later.

### Student Deliverables

- Phase 1 evidence folder
- Personal reflection
- Best artifact demo
- Baseline scorecard
- Phase 2 improvement plan

### Mentor Checks

- Has the student completed the minimum evidence?
- Did they improve over six days?
- Are they coachable?
- What role might suit them later?
- What support do they need next week?

### AI Monitoring Input

- All Phase 1 artifacts
- Reflection
- Scorecard
- Mentor notes
- Recommended support plan

## Phase 1 Review Questions

Before approving this D1-D6 plan, check:

- Is Day 1 too abstract, or does it create enough action?
- Is Day 2 field observation feasible with the available training location?
- Is Day 4's tiny build too early, or is it useful for confidence?
- Is Day 5 team pressure too soon, or necessary to reveal behavior?
- Is the Day 6 gate supportive enough rather than exam-like?
- Do we need parent orientation during these six days?
- Should students be allowed to use phones for field documentation?
- What tools must be installed before Day 1 to avoid wasting time?
