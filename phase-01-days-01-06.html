Phase 1 | Days 1-6

Awakening and baseline

The first six days reset how students think about engineering, AI, problem solving, teamwork, and proof of work. This phase should not rush into startups or software. It should reveal who can observe, question, verify, communicate, and finish useful work.

6 daysfoundation sprint
60 hours10 hours per day
1 gatereadiness review

Why this phase exists

Break college habits before building ventures.

Many students arrive trained to wait for instructions, search for the right answer, copy examples, avoid uncertainty, and use AI as an answer machine. Phase 1 should break those habits gently but firmly.

By Day 6, every student should have:

  • A personal baseline profile
  • A working AI learning and execution setup
  • A daily build log habit
  • A first visible evidence artifact on Day 1
  • A first tiny useful real-world build by Day 4
  • A problem observation notebook with at least 15 real-world observations
  • Experience working in rotating mini-teams
  • A readiness score for Phase 2

Before Day 1

Do not waste the first training day on logistics.

The academy team should prepare accounts, sheets, mentor scoring, communication groups, challenge sheets, artifact submission, and photo/video permissions before students enter the room.

Detailed D1-D6 flow

Each day has a core question, visible output, and mentor check.

D1

Wake up to the new reality

Foundation day plus baseline day

Core question: Why is ordinary engineering education not enough anymore?

Objectives

  • Establish the seriousness and opportunity of the AI wave
  • Explain that the course is problem-first, not software-first
  • Capture baseline thinking, communication, and AI usage ability
  • Start the daily evidence habit

Student outputs

  • Learning contract
  • Personal reflection note
  • Messy problem breakdown sheet
  • AI verification worksheet
  • Day 1 build log
Suggested case:

A small fabrication shop is losing money because jobs are delayed, material is wasted, and customers keep calling for status updates. Students must avoid jumping to "build software" and identify possible causes first.

Open full Day 1 plan
D2

Learn to observe before solving

Foundation day plus field-preparation day

Core question: Can you see problems clearly before trying to solve them?

Objectives

  • Teach observation as an engineering skill
  • Separate symptom, cause, constraint, and opportunity
  • Notice workflows, delays, waste, confusion, and repeated manual effort

Student outputs

  • 10 raw observations
  • 3 structured problem anatomy sheets
  • Question bank improved with AI and human filtering
  • 90-second problem presentation
Observation targets:

Workshop tool storage, lab equipment usage, canteen queue, repair work, small shop inventory, fabrication job tracking, transport, project procurement, admission processes.

D3

AI as coworker, not answer machine

Skill sprint day

Core question: Can you use AI to think better without becoming dependent on it?

Objectives

  • Teach AI delegation, verification, and iteration
  • Show why AI answers are often incomplete for real-world problems
  • Use AI for questions, risks, alternatives, and documentation

Student outputs

  • AI error log
  • Personal AI workflow template
  • Verification evidence
  • Revised problem note using AI responsibly
AI output is a draft. Ask for questions before solutions. Verify important claims.
D4

First tiny real-world build

Skill sprint day plus build day

Core question: Can you create a useful artifact in one day?

Acceptable tiny builds

  • Checklist for a repeated workshop process
  • Job tracking board template
  • Cost estimation spreadsheet
  • Maintenance log format
  • Simple inspection form
  • AI-generated but human-verified SOP

Not acceptable

  • Generic chatbot
  • Generic todo app
  • Landing page without real problem evidence
  • AI-generated report with no verification
  • Pretty slides with no operational usefulness
Mentor check:

Is it connected to a real observation? Could a real person use it? Is it too complicated? Did the student test it lightly?

D5

Teamwork, roles, and communication under pressure

Build day plus review day

Core question: Can students work with others without hiding, dominating, or waiting?

Rotating roles

  • Problem owner
  • Reality checker
  • AI operator
  • Builder or documenter
  • Presenter
  • Cost/time estimator

Student outputs

  • Team problem brief
  • Team artifact
  • Individual contribution log
  • Peer feedback
  • Team demo and reflection
Mentor check:

Who takes initiative? Who listens? Who hides? Who dominates? Who turns messy discussion into action?

D6

Baseline review and Phase 2 readiness gate

Review day plus gate day

Core question: Is each student ready to enter deeper problem-solving work?

Scorecard

  • Curiosity and observation quality
  • Problem framing and verification habit
  • AI usage maturity
  • Communication and team behavior
  • Reliability and coachability

Gate decision

  • Ready: proceeds normally
  • Needs support: proceeds with extra clinic work
  • At risk: gets a probation or parent/student conversation
Student outputs:

Phase 1 evidence folder, personal reflection, best artifact demo, baseline scorecard, and Phase 2 improvement plan.

Review before approving

Questions to test whether D1-D6 makes sense.

Is Day 1 too abstract, or does it create enough action?
Is Day 2 field observation feasible with the available training location?
Is Day 4's tiny build too early, or useful for confidence?
Is Day 5 team pressure too soon, or necessary to reveal behavior?
Is the Day 6 gate supportive enough rather than exam-like?
Do we need parent orientation during these six days?
Should students use phones for field documentation?
What tools must be installed before Day 1?
